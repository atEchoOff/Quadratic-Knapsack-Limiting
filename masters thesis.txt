:Abstract<br><br>Entropy stable nodal discontinuous Galerkin methods satisfy a cell entropy inequality. One strategy to enforce such an inequality is through knapsack limiting, which solves a knapsack problem to determine an optimal set of limiting coefficients which result in a semi-discrete cell entropy inequality while preserving nodal bounds. In this work, we provide a slight modification of this approach, where we utilize a quadratic knapsack problem instead of a standard linear knapsack problem. We prove that this quadratic knapsack problem can be reduced to efficient scalar root-finding. Numerical results demonstrate that the proposed quadratic knapsack limiting strategy is efficient, results in better time accuracy than the linear knapsack limiting approach, and behaves better under adaptive time-stepping.<br><br>:Introduction and Notation<br><br>In a general conservation law, we may have multiple conserved variables. For notation, I will denote&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="RrZ1pvu2TrUgszLnHiUX" name="btext-math-input">\mathbbF=\mathbbR^n</span></div><u>, where&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="5akO3umpHmGbtu49y8fG" name="btext-math-input">n</span></div><u>&nbsp;are the number of conserved variables.&nbsp;</u>We consider a convervation law of the form<br><br><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="nJHaWRGZl4F7Y8gKeF4K" name="btext-math-input">\frac{du}{dt}+\frac{d}{dx}\left(f\left(u\right)\right)=0</span></div>&nbsp;(1)<br><br>for a solution&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="31SCjlWNxeeHxTB45MUx" name="btext-math-input">u:\mathbbR^d\times\mathbbR_{\ge0}\to\mathbbF</span></div><u>, where&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="oYHGwuqiqmGm4oCtoEKa" name="btext-math-input">d</span></div><u>&nbsp;is the number of dimensions in our solution, </u><u>with&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="3t2m9NMZhSnpg9S1glTt" name="btext-math-input">f:\mathbbF\to\mathbbF</span></div><u>&nbsp;the corresponding flux function.&nbsp;</u><u>For the sake of simplicity, we will focus on one-dimensional solutions for now, with the note that the extension to multiple dimensions is simple, and explained in Chans and Lin's paper. Therefore, we seek solutions of the form&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="i4jKxGL6FDaUTWVrmWby" name="btext-math-input">u:\mathbbR\times\mathbbR_{\ge0}\to\mathbbF</span></div><u>. We discretize our solution into&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="FbdeiRKT9QEPoDh5w99l" name="btext-math-input">M\in\mathbbN</span></div><u>&nbsp;distinct, equally spaced elements, and consider the&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="oWFcauaBgIFcyoKutVD8" name="btext-math-input">N\in\mathbbN</span></div><u>&nbsp;degree polynomial approximation over each element. We will seek to enforce an entropy inequality over each of these elements. Consider a general element, and denote&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="PYKRuiqbufOaX1x2l4qS" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{u}\in\mathbbF^{N+1}</span></div><u>&nbsp;the spatially discretized solution.&nbsp;</u><br><br>Let&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="9td7uMiBVWrH4Xt52yVp" name="btext-math-input">\left(\eta,F\right)</span></div>&nbsp;be a convex entropy-entropy flux pair, with&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="PL1Uf2Ial6PQI6z9ZR9C" name="btext-math-input">\eta:\mathbbF\to\mathbbF</span></div><u>&nbsp;and&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="PK6EssWQYCi8p0u6sw8v" name="btext-math-input">F:\mathbbF\to\mathbbR</span></div>. We say that the solution is entropy conservative if<br><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="HtbRluKhFSzDXkLNiVgS" name="btext-math-input">\frac{d\eta\left(u\right)}{dt}+\frac{dF\left(u\right)}{dx}\le0</span></div><u>&nbsp;(2)<br></u><br>However, we generally approximate this condition by integrating over the element&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="qvkvKEnsAuAG8U5WznWx" name="btext-math-input">\Omega</span></div><u>. We therefore aim to satisfy<br></u><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="jVdRdfYhjENHkciuZRzw" name="btext-math-input">\int_{\Omega}^{ }\frac{d\eta\left(u\right)}{dt}+\int_{\Omega}^{ }\frac{dF\left(u\right)}{dx}\le0</span></div><br><br>Utilizing the built-in quadrature for nodal DG methods, we therefore have<br><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="RXavPIu2bOWUX81DZZkZ" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{v}^T\fnt{M}\cdot\frac{d\fnt{u}}{dt}\le F\left(\fnt{u}_1\right)-F\left(\fnt{u}_{N+1}\right)</span></div><u><br></u><br>where&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="VHItu4HnWfr0XVS5No5j" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{M}\in\mathbbR^{\left(N+1\right)\times\left(N+1\right)}</span></div><u>&nbsp;denotes the mass matrix, and&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="j2lieoaDiKkgyIICnTOa" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{v}\in\mathbbF^{N+1}</span></div><u>&nbsp;denotes the discretized entropy variables,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="JRJAETe3KOVHY41jFHh5" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{v}=\nabla\fnt{\eta}\left(\fnt{u}\right)</span></div><u>. As&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="CVekWq2q5zaoH7jNePNS" name="btext-math-input">\left(\eta,F\right)</span></div><u>&nbsp;is an entropy-entropy flux pair, we often substitute&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="vtY1QVLJQuJBn2uCACGE" name="btext-math-input">F\left(u\right)=v^Tf^{\ast}\left(u,u^+\right)-\psi\left(u\right)</span></div><u>, where&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="iXVll0drgYO1cin4S2uF" name="btext-math-input">f^{\ast}</span></div><u>&nbsp;denotes the numerical flux, and&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="ZsfSqs0GlMZUqlARwkE6" name="btext-math-input">\psi:\mathbbF\to\mathbbR</span></div><u>&nbsp;is defined&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="jK2go0krCUWMg6xlAEKi" name="btext-math-input">\psi\left(u\right)=v^Tf^{\ast}\left(u,u^+\right)-F\left(u\right)</span></div><u>. This gives us our local entropy inequality,&nbsp;<br></u><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="4U05JYnYmpjzRb0mxnxx" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{v}^T\fnt{M}\cdot\frac{d\fnt{u}}{dt}\le\left[\psi\left(\fnt{u}_{N+1}\right)-\psi\left(\fnt{u}_1\right)\right]-\left[\fnt{v}_{N+1}^Tf^{\ast}\left(\fnt{u}_{N+1},\fnt{u}_{N+1}^+\right)-\fnt{v}_1^Tf^{\ast}\left(\fnt{u}_1,\fnt{u}_1^+\right)\right]</span></div>&nbsp;(3)<br><br>:Background<br><br>Here, I will describe the method taken in Yimin Lin and Jesse Chan's paper, in 1-dimensional space. We utilize a low order and high order method with the following properties: the low order method satisfies the entropy inequality described in (3), the low order method is positivity preserving, both the low and high order methods satisfy local conservation of the conserved variables, and the high order method is high order accurate. At a single timestep, we utilize the low and high order methods to generate&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="ivXbjQSwbuDWdZiz4Dgf" name="btext-math-input">\frac{d\fnt{u}^L}{dt}\in\mathbbF^{N+1}</span></div><u>, the low order update, and&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="0nr48MRjGJEPEdS50qLl" name="btext-math-input">\frac{d\fnt{u}^H}{dt}\in\mathbbF^{N+1}</span></div><u>, the high order update.&nbsp;</u><u>Yimin and Jesse's paper show exactly how to massage the low order scheme to satisfy these properties in a simple manner. </u><u>We then define&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="oy9jw9pvI9KCcgTPFX5S" name="btext-math-input">\overline{f}_0^H,...,\overline{f}_{N+1}^H\in\mathbbF</span></div><u>&nbsp;and&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="A9F4EMfpEHDl10ZrQSRc" name="btext-math-input">\overline{f}_0^L,...,\overline{f}_{N+1}^L\in\mathbbF</span></div><u>&nbsp;as follows:<br></u><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="xfe2VBtr4y4ix4yY0lgq" name="btext-math-input">\begin{tmatrix}\overline{f}_0^H=f^{\ast}\left(u_1,u_1^+\right)&amp;f_0^L=f^{\ast}\left(u_1,u_1^+\right)\\\frac{d\fnt{u}_i^H}{dt}\fnt{M}_{ii}=\overline{f}_i^H-\overline{f}_{i-1}^H&amp;\frac{d\fnt{u}_i^L}{dt}\fnt{M}_{ii}=\overline{f}_i^L-\overline{f}_{i-1}^L\end{tmatrix}</span></div><u><br></u><br>for all&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="dBqjgnESKD4bN6TQgZQ7" name="btext-math-input">i\in\left\{1,...,N+1\right\}</span></div><u>. Then, given&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="Kdive4RxYpVgyx1XAXNn" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\theta}\in\mathbbR^{N+2}</span></div><u>, we define the optimized update,<br></u><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="922jOKiIqpCAJCdWRtOe" name="btext-math-input">\frac{d\fnt{u}_i}{dt}\fnt{M}_{ii}=\overline{f}_i\left(\fnt{\theta}_i\right)-\overline{f}_{i-1}\left(\fnt{\theta}_{i-1}\right)</span></div><u><br></u><br>where<br><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="pv9LbZqewcwmBp6l66EX" name="btext-math-input">\overline{f}_i\left(\fnt{\theta}_i\right)=\fnt{\theta}_i\overline{f}_i^H+\left(1-\fnt{\theta}_i\right)\overline{f}_i^L</span></div><u><br></u><br>Then, since the resulting update is a convex combination of the low and high order updates, we have that the optimized update must satisfy conservation.&nbsp;<br><br>Since the low order scheme is known to satisfy positivity, we can generate&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="X2oJvJbuWa8f1NFToYzt" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\ell^c}\in\left[0,1\right]^{N+2}</span></div><u>&nbsp;in such a way that if&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="HTEMN08p0vBZ2h9yD0JY" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\theta}\in\left[\fnt{0},\fnt{\ell^c}\right]</span></div><u>, then the resulting update satisfies positivity.&nbsp;</u><u><br></u><br>Lin and Chan were able to show that the entropy condition (3) can then be simplified into<br><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="SkkiKVQHLR20TT2ebLWM" name="btext-math-input">\sum_{i=1}^{N+2}\left(\fnt{v}_i-\fnt{v}_{i+1}\right)^T\left(\overline{f}_i^H-\overline{f}_i^L\right)\cdot\fnt{\theta}_i\le\left[\psi\left(\fnt{u}_{N+1}\right)-\psi\left(\fnt{u}_1\right)\right]-\sum_{i=1}^{N+2}\left(\fnt{v}_i-\fnt{v}_{i+1}\right)^Tf_i^L</span></div><u><br></u><br>If we denote&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="FKb3bz1efgnX3n3S9r3u" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}\in\mathbbR^{N+2}</span></div><u>&nbsp;such that&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="5lnZZDuKsSiEnWkwNb3R" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}_i=\left(\fnt{v}_i-\fnt{v}_{i+1}\right)^T\left(\overline{f}_i^H-\overline{f}_i^L\right)</span></div><u>, and let&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="zrw3uYgcmgUK6yxIX9At" equivalent to the simplified entropy inequality&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="dOAf16xDg201SPqfK6IZ" All of this together hints that we want to determine&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="dbqVCWBCanBNOK3ThtHZ" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\theta}\in\mathbbR^{N+2}</span></div><u>&nbsp;such that&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="EjWrXOYDN8WRO6VMoxYN" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\theta}\le b</span></div><u>,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="I9b26Becbuf3n5AnxrCM" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{0}\le\fnt{\theta}\le\fnt{\ell^c}</span></div><u>, and we want&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="Xf46Fh0aw8wwEvzngHDm" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\theta}</span></div><u>&nbsp;to be as close to&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="tPlkFySiPiX5lEprKF3G" name="btext-math-input">\mathbbOne</span></div><u>&nbsp;as possible, so that&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="6x1pjfKy8MK2DerRjAPm" name="btext-math-input">\frac{d\fnt{u}}{dt}</span></div><u>&nbsp;is as close to&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="Yn8lB67hido0IXbEvjEb" name="btext-math-input">\frac{d\fnt{u}^H}{dt}</span></div><u>&nbsp;as possible. Lin and Chan chose to apply the linear knapsack problem here:<br></u><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="VOgReVX4Ul6vpdVu9Fgi" name="btext-math-input">\max_{\begin{tmatrix}\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\theta}\le b\\\fnt{0}\le\fnt{\theta}\le\fnt{\ell^c}\end{tmatrix}}\mathbbOne^T\fnt{\theta}</span></div><u><br><br>Last, Lin and Chan were able to show that the above problem is feasible, assuming the problem is well-posed. Note that by linearity of the objective function, we can instead solve the above problem as a minimization problem:<br><br></u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="3TVTkN2wPAKRLFrNM6J5" name="btext-math-input">\arg\max_{\begin{tmatrix}\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\theta}\le b\\\fnt{0}\le\fnt{\theta}\le\fnt{\ell^c}\end{tmatrix}}\mathbbOne^T\fnt{\theta}=\ell^c-\arg\min_{_{\begin{tmatrix}\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\theta}\le b\\\fnt{0}\le\fnt{\theta}\le\fnt{\ell^c}\end{tmatrix}}}\mathbbOne^T\fnt{\theta}</span></div><u><br><br>where&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="Za5HDwYkwnI1MAvLk2WQ" name="btext-math-input">\tilde{b}=\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\ell^c}-b</span></div><u>.&nbsp;</u>Therefore, we can equivalently consider the minimization problem.&nbsp;The linear knapsack problem is beneficial because there is a very simple, efficient, and easily debuggable algorithm for the solution of the above problem. This is equivalent to optimizing over the&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="a9ONNi9sgjM9UhEak5FR" name="btext-math-input">L^1</span></div><u>&nbsp;norm of the&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="0G4MPn9QDcEKppFUA8Ve" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\theta}</span></div>&nbsp;vector. <br><br>::The Quadratic Knapsack Problem<br><br>As mathematicians, we have an unhealthy, though not unfounded, obsession towards the&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="6iN5erbdr2t4N7iR6s4S" name="btext-math-input">L^2</span></div><u>&nbsp;norm. This work aims to explore both a method of solving the quadratic knapsack problem<br></u><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="IQBhDtgz7q24veDdNqiP" name="btext-math-input">\min_{\begin{tmatrix}\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\theta}\ge\tilde{b}\\\fnt{0}\le\fnt{\theta}\le\fnt{\ell^c}\end{tmatrix}}\fnt{\theta}^T\fnt{W}\fnt{\theta}</span></div><u>&nbsp;(QP)<br></u><br>where&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="KDoahY70dM8dooRrlIbl" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{W}=\diag\left(\fnt{w}\right)</span></div><u>&nbsp;and&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="xmv5uixTgPrB7whfNNfO" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{w}\in\mathbbR_{&gt;0}^N</span></div><u>. Then,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="Yt3ZyXonXF1VZ0kH4avi" name="btext-math-input">\left‖\fnt{\theta}\right‖_{\fnt{W}}^2=\fnt{\theta}^T\fnt{W}\fnt{\theta}</span></div><u>&nbsp;describes a weighted&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="lFM6OIIRaqz9sZneCYgm" name="btext-math-input">L^2</span></div><u>&nbsp;norm. Using this problem is equivalently beneficial as using the&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="V8LOjWQBGZmXKmlNRVRM" name="btext-math-input">L^1</span></div>&nbsp;knapsack problem: we aim to minimize the&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="hURpbceynCoVupeZpX2A" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\theta}</span></div><u>&nbsp;vector in some sense, subject to it satisfying some positivity constraint&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="oHwXGoa8piH8tzmQlAZT" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{0}\le\fnt{\theta}\le\fnt{\ell^c}</span></div><u>, and some entropy inequality&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="1TI7eJEMqKGHZ8XdIPJq" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\theta}\ge\tilde{b}</span></div><u>. However, we must first discuss how to solve such a problem algorithmically. Consider the restriction,<br><br></u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="SGRrKtHcBe6E422E6VD4" name="btext-math-input">\min_{\begin{tmatrix}\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\theta}=\tilde{b}\\\fnt{0}\le\fnt{\theta}\le\fnt{\ell^c}\end{tmatrix}}\fnt{\theta}^T\fnt{W}\fnt{\theta}</span></div><u>&nbsp;(QEP)</u><br><br><b>Theorem:</b>&nbsp;If&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="gwfEYLznfuP7dYiBdquQ" name="btext-math-input">\tilde{b}&gt;0</span></div><u>, (QP) and (QEP) have the same unique solution.&nbsp;<br></u><br><b>Proof:</b>&nbsp;Note first that the objective function&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="ecPvZlCNHoH4fuMsHpOJ" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\theta}^T\fnt{W}\fnt{\theta}</span></div><u>&nbsp;is strictly convex. Moreover, the feasible spaces for (QP) and (QEP) are both convex. Therefore, (QP) and (QEP) must have a unique solution. Denote them&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="XzYV0fNk8zsBbYLfkxbR" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast},\fnt{y}_{\ast}\in\mathbbR^{N+2}</span></div><u>&nbsp;respectively. Suppose for the sake of contradiction that&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="dQXizMf2MKBWBRD13wfR" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}^T\fnt{x}_{\ast}&gt;\tilde{b}</span></div><u>.&nbsp;</u><u>Then,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="e2VmjYGhrlUWlkuGpIXv" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}^T\fnt{x}_{\ast}&gt;\tilde{b}&gt;0</span></div><u>. So, there must be some&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="GDBWl31THg5i1c3CZFi3" name="btext-math-input">i\in\left\{1,...,N+2\right\}</span></div><u>&nbsp;such that&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="C3BWAQdR1lRnUpyofu5a" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}_i,\left(\fnt{x}_{\ast}\right)_i&gt;0</span></div><u>. Then, there must exist some&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="QTyvzWfNVhJxvCxcjEOE" name="btext-math-input">\varepsilon&gt;0</span></div><u>&nbsp;so that&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="ko6R0QnuemyICb3GsGWA" name="btext-math-input">\left(\fnt{x}_{\ast}\right)_i&gt;\varepsilon</span></div><u>&nbsp;and&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="qYibdgFJ8tpMVDVMRERW" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}^T\fnt{x}_{\ast}-\fnt{a}_i\varepsilon&gt;\tilde{b}</span></div><u>. Consider&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="687ISjR9Og8KGHjgS56z" name="btext-math-input">\tilde{\fnt{x}}_{\ast}\in\mathbbR^{N+2}</span></div><u>&nbsp;which remains the same as&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="dSQb8DEJv6sJvP2juHts" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast}</span></div>, except that&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="UiuYheg4TuWXgTn54n0U" name="btext-math-input">\left(\tilde{\fnt{x}}_{\ast}\right)_i=\left(\fnt{x}_{\ast}\right)_i-\varepsilon</span></div><u>. Then,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="ml6qcHxMZjgwDSD5wGWS" name="btext-math-input">0\le\left(\tilde{\fnt{x}}_{\ast}\right)_i=\left(\fnt{x}_{\ast}\right)_i-\varepsilon&lt;\left(\fnt{x}_{\ast}\right)_i\le\left(\fnt{\ell^c}\right)_i</span></div><u>, and&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="3YefVy3AFCObx7eTJah8" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}^T\tilde{\fnt{x}}_{\ast}=\fnt{a}^T\fnt{x}_{\ast}-\fnt{a}_i\varepsilon&gt;\tilde{b}</span></div><u>. Therefore,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="qAHDuxfK2GTEhS8CR2I3" name="btext-math-input">\tilde{\fnt{x}}_{\ast}</span></div><u>&nbsp;is feasible for (QP). Moreover, since&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="HXdxKsh60AVCUi5Bcsfl" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{0}&lt;\left(\tilde{\fnt{x}}_{\ast}\right)_i&lt;\left(\fnt{x}_{\ast}\right)_i</span></div><u>, and&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="DO1CJ2mPmASYEi3fLmzy" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{w}&gt;0</span></div><u>,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="lTqozXXuzIxOBr79tSsv" name="btext-math-input">\tilde{\fnt{x}}_{\ast}^T\fnt{W}\tilde{\fnt{x}}_{\ast}&lt;\fnt{x}_{\ast}^T\fnt{W}\fnt{x}_{\ast}</span></div><u>. This contradicts the optimality of&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="iDzF22T0bFogdAaIoNkm" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast}</span></div><u>&nbsp;for (QP). Therefore, we arrive at a contradiction, and&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="rU5KHqOG7Lw1UlXQcupv" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}^T\fnt{x}_{\ast}=\tilde{b}</span></div><u>. Hence,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="mEaRtRUKe55SSX6GeYYp" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast}</span></div><u>&nbsp;is feasible for (QEP). By the optimality of&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="deXUStchC8l6sCfZPALq" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{y}_{\ast}</span></div><u>&nbsp;for (QEP),&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="9QrjYUgC2kyj14KCYdn0" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{y}_{\ast}^T\fnt{W}\fnt{y}_{\ast}\le\fnt{x}_{\ast}^T\fnt{W}\fnt{x}_{\ast}</span></div><u>. Since the feasible region for (QEP) is a subset of that for (QP), we also have that&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="8P6uzNJ3ask2GHFEB5St" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{y}_{\ast}</span></div><u>&nbsp;is feasible for (QP). Therefore, we must have that&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="4UYZj4nfihNabvvmtH1L" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{y}_{\ast}</span></div><u>&nbsp;is optimal for (QP). By the uniqueness of an optimal solution for (QP),&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="IDEJQtnZTvjOIJVTATot" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast}=\fnt{y}_{\ast}</span></div><u>.&nbsp;<br><br></u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="P1i2Jih1Hk79lEeLQZsk" name="btext-math-input">\square</span></div><u></u><br><br><b>Remark:</b>&nbsp;If&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="1r9HW9BNCcyitmWuVqnE" name="btext-math-input">\tilde{b}\le0</span></div><u>, then&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="biIAsxVpb87fgyzCrs1J" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\theta}=\fnt{0}</span></div><u>&nbsp;is optimal for (QP).<br><br><b>Proof:</b>&nbsp;This is because&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="PRd54kWLCoTnFktBvPIr" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\theta}=\fnt{0}</span></div><u>&nbsp;optimizes the objective function globally, and&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="xxq06pZXqrAKqZuGMpfK" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\theta}=\fnt{0}</span></div><u>&nbsp;is feasible in the case where&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="wQGzuQ7jiWc9Po7SLttT" name="btext-math-input">\tilde{b}\le0</span></div><u>. We now can reduce solving (QP) to solving (QEP), which has a nicer solution. <br><br></u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="7HivK61GxWKuEE5ESZev" name="btext-math-input">\square</span></div><u></u><br><u><br>Consider the parameterized problem:</u><br><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="VtqmA6YZT5TrFurw3Gxd" name="btext-math-input">\min_{\fnt{0}\le\fnt{\theta}\le\fnt{\ell^c}}\left(\frac{1}{2}\fnt{\theta}^T\fnt{W}\fnt{\theta}+\lambda\left(\tilde{b}-\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\theta}\right)\right)</span></div><u>&nbsp;(PQEP)</u><u><br></u><br>for&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="7y3rkykzYQJLJTgxrulM" name="btext-math-input">\lambda\in\mathbbR</span></div><u>. The solution for the above problem is&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="OP4ZDkVm49P7IdKhs7TM" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\theta}\left(\lambda\right)=\clip_{\fnt{0,\ell^c}}\left(\lambda\fnt{W}^{-1}\fnt{a}\right)</span></div>, where for&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="lvEz4TGO53IOtuAX4WmR" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x},\fnt{y},\fnt{z}\in\mathbbR^{N+2}</span></div>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="JnVeQHlm3BbzIGFGdcBb" name="btext-math-input">i\in\left\{1,...,N+2\right\}</span></div><u>, we define&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="32ZLZS9XGDCRbVm1pgAq" name="btext-math-input">\clip_{\fnt{x},\fnt{y}}\left(\fnt{z}\right)_i=\begin{cmatrix}\fnt{x}_i&amp;\fnt{z}_i&lt;\fnt{x}_i\\\fnt{y}_i&amp;\fnt{z}_i&gt;\fnt{y}_i\\\fnt{z}_i&amp;\text{otherwise}\end{cmatrix}</span></div><u>. This describes an elementwise clipping of the given vector, between two vectors.&nbsp;</u><br><br><b>Theorem:</b>&nbsp;If&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="zV45aqeJwodJpSLRXm9g" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\theta}\left(\lambda\right)=\tilde{b}</span></div>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="7PzBOld74kwJ9449oniT" name="btext-math-input">\tilde{b}&gt;0</span></div>, then&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="FjH3IbOjM9K8zPB0EfXp" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast}=\fnt{\theta}\left(\lambda\right)</span></div><u>&nbsp;solves (QP).&nbsp;</u><br><br><b>Proof:</b> Let&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="cpNARqwlOh2RorsvPmTz" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}\in\mathbbR^{N+2}</span></div><u>&nbsp;be feasible for (QEP). Then,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="etWQOro2JYCQ7IcKUnds" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}^T\fnt{x}=\tilde{b}</span></div><u>. Moreover,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="d7UvAFt9lhknviytBpCE" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}</span></div><u>&nbsp;is feasible for (PQEP)</u><u>. Therefore, by the optimality of&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="0jbPmzrKDvb23ZRoLY8h" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast}</span></div>, we have<br><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="3BBTA8AD03hhQphAcJlY" name="btext-math-input">\frac{1}{2}\fnt{x}_{\ast}^T\fnt{W}\fnt{x}_{\ast}+\lambda\left(\tilde{b}-\fnt{a}^T\fnt{x}_{\ast}\right)\le\frac{1}{2}\fnt{x}^T\fnt{W}\fnt{x}+\lambda\left(\tilde{b}-\fnt{a}^T\fnt{x}\right)</span></div><u><br></u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="Nc6lLQUQlIuYcXXmDFVq" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast}^T\fnt{W}\fnt{x}_{\ast}\le\fnt{x}^T\fnt{W}\fnt{x}</span></div><u><br></u><br>Since&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="kOEWQUuoZEobfEMV4knw" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}</span></div>, feasible for (QEP) is arbitrary, and since&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="L0fExrWBYj2EdT1pd7G4" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast}</span></div><u>&nbsp;is feasible for (QEP),&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="IDFGYiiOKlOZugXjtP7K" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast}</span></div><u>&nbsp;is optimal for (QEP). Therefore, since&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="thbsZtJrzpUbQNPOdmjA" name="btext-math-input">\tilde{b}&gt;0</span></div><u>,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="p4Ps5IhW8PnlYsn0zgCA" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast}</span></div><u>&nbsp;solves (QP) by theorem 1.&nbsp;<br><br></u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="3rdDIkc5HfeM7SOd9MYj" name="btext-math-input">\square</span></div><u></u><br><br><b>Theorem:</b>&nbsp;If&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="JXduyvr756XohSCq7LQF" name="btext-math-input">\tilde{b}&gt;0</span></div><u>, there is some&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="KtuOwXTMKjnhqBnLdJfH" name="btext-math-input">\lambda&gt;0</span></div><u>&nbsp;such&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="m0Xz3czM5MttfbzDXXEF" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast}=\fnt{\theta}\left(\lambda\right)</span></div><u>&nbsp;solves (QP).&nbsp;<br></u><br><b>Proof:</b> Denote&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="sSCT7WWnHLswWvZuhjSY" name="btext-math-input">f:\left[0,\infty\right)\to\mathbbR</span></div><u>&nbsp;so that&nbsp;</u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="mNykgs0KOrZa7a2F8J5W" name="btext-math-input">f\left(\lambda\right)=\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\theta}\left(\lambda\right)-\tilde{b}</span></div><u>. Therefore,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="wBbKR6Riwyh30kWUJlcu" name="btext-math-input">f</span></div><u>&nbsp;is continuous. Moreover,&nbsp;<br></u><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="ueRFz6eyOZp9j55YWuE4" name="btext-math-input">f\left(0\right)=\sum_{i=1}^{N+2}\fnt{a}_i\clip_{\fnt{0},\fnt{\ell^c}_i}\left(\frac{\lambda\fnt{a}_i}{\fnt{w}_i}\right)-\tilde{b}</span></div><u><br></u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="JBnLXGfSyXlaPe8MgcTD" name="btext-math-input">=-\tilde{b}&lt;0</span></div><u><br></u><br>Moreover, notice<br><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="3TFMwvsDWSQz5w7gqb0d" name="btext-math-input">\lim_{\lambda\to\infty}f\left(\lambda\right)=\sum_{i=1}^{N+2}\fnt{a}_i\lim_{\lambda\to\infty}\clip_{\fnt{0},\fnt{\ell^c}_i}\left(\frac{\lambda\fnt{a}_i}{\fnt{w}_i}\right)-\tilde{b}</span></div><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="hOv5wlZDCkn4VPhVqNoi" name="btext-math-input">=\sum_{\begin{tmatrix}i=1\\\fnt{a}_i&gt;0\end{tmatrix}}^{N+2}\fnt{a}_i\fnt{\ell^c}_i-\tilde{b}</span></div><u><br></u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="TzBBPdz4vOOdzoRKifMj" name="btext-math-input">\ge\sum_{i=1}^{N+2}\fnt{a}_i\fnt{\ell^c}_i-\tilde{b}</span></div><u><br></u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="jbsTVn04VogSBdeoNjmN" name="btext-math-input">=\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\ell^c}-\left(\fnt{a}^T\fnt{\ell^c}-b\right)</span></div><br><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="EXizjRI0i4In0QnqOxmt" name="btext-math-input">=b</span></div><u><br></u><br>Lin and Chan showed that the low order solution is feasible for the knapsack problem. The low order solution corresponds to&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="qZgaIDkXBGgaMhWnsexf" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{\theta}=\fnt{0}</span></div><u>. Therefore,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="OPdDNXAzHDdaQLJJeQ9f" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{a}^T\fnt{0}\le b</span></div><u>, so&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="0XEAYrdbQX5vcvMeUHGk" name="btext-math-input">b\ge0</span></div><u>. Notice by above,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="zUS4ZXkqI6oct56j8947" name="btext-math-input">\lim_{\lambda\to\infty}f\left(\lambda\right)=\sum_{\begin{tmatrix}i=1\\\fnt{a}_i&gt;0\end{tmatrix}}^{N+2}\fnt{a}_i\fnt{\ell^c}_i-\tilde{b}&lt;\infty</span></div><u>. Therefore, since&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="8fEw3ZEYNxexaONDTowQ" name="btext-math-input">f</span></div><u>&nbsp;is piecewise linear, the limit as&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="QwdYctQbT5MFXuQQU71I" name="btext-math-input">\lambda\to\infty</span></div><u>&nbsp;must be achieved at some&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="zdRCdxwEQ9ZVWBXWvIXZ" name="btext-math-input">\mu&gt;0</span></div><u>. Therefore,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="7ZjUGhHDD2r0HLKxQM3P" name="btext-math-input">f\left(\mu\right)\ge0</span></div><u>. Therefore, by the definition of the iteration&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="eRmESW9gjc1cwpPLcNyh" name="btext-math-input">\lambda_{k+1}=\lambda_k-\frac{f\left(\lambda_k\right)}{\delta f\left(\lambda_k\right)}</span></div><u>&nbsp;and since&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="UGrx4MPIe3JhF1VXgdMp" name="btext-math-input">\delta f</span></div><u>&nbsp;is a nonnegative function, we also have&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="YvcC074qyrNvcNXxoidA" name="btext-math-input">\lambda_k\le\lambda_{k+1}</span></div><u>&nbsp;for all&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="uAUQmgn0gVH6nOWhkFj8" name="btext-math-input">k</span></div><u>&nbsp;in the iteration.<br><br></u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="XSs5olETDPIKSan2wyIw" name="btext-math-input">\square</span></div><br><u><br>Denote&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="4B7FiNur8lubTLD3psWU" name="btext-math-input">d\left(\lambda\right)=\left|\left\{x\in\left[0,\lambda\right):f\ \text{is nondifferentiable at}\ x\right\}\right|</span></div><u>. That is,&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="T2iWrhxfybcQrer6jjC2" name="btext-math-input">d\left(\lambda\right)</span></div><u>&nbsp;is the number of nondifferentiabilities strictly to the left of&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="NLCCOFgMWBwSLuVjrwlu" name="btext-math-input">\lambda</span></div><u>.</u><br><br><b>Theorem:</b>&nbsp;If&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="VrxRWafUQplHfRf9Q2JB" name="btext-math-input">\tilde{b}&gt;0</span></div><u>, the pseudo-Newton iteration converges in at most&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="DbtRVFMtahqYPH85AVfO" name="btext-math-input">d\left(\infty\right)</span></div><u>&nbsp;</u><u>iterations to the root&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="OsrlkhWJKarfqV4aP7cL" name="btext-math-input">\lambda_{\ast}</span></div><u>&nbsp;such that&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="edcxRNIfDyHUW9gamFyJ" name="btext-math-input">\fnt{x}_{\ast}=\fnt{\theta}\left(\lambda_{\ast}\right)</span></div><u>&nbsp;is the solution of (QP).&nbsp;<br></u><br>Proof: Since&nbsp;<u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="iZ9HFSNuFLuTeyugyyQ9" name="btext-math-input">\tilde{b}&gt;0</span></div><u>, the results from theorem 5 hold. Since&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="rqbFyt778GAXIervgEkP" name="btext-math-input">f</span></div><u>&nbsp;is linear on&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="6190762j4q7RuqsdIYhW" name="btext-math-input">\left[\lambda_k,\lambda_{k+1}\right]</span></div><u>, we have that&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="cr8YiZV9wIWUHPRw9nV5" name="btext-math-input">f=T</span></div><u>&nbsp;on&nbsp;</u><u></u><div name='math-container' style='display: inline-block;' contenteditable=false><span id="0bGi8HuT7jpsaRXWb65X" name="btext-math-input">\left[\lambda_k,\lambda_{k+1}\right]</span></div><u>.
